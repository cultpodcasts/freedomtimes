# Using *cult* in Europe & UK Cult News roundups

Canonical wording guide for weekly reports (`type: roundup`). Agents and editors should read this before drafting or proofreading.

Related: [WEEKLY_REPORT_WRITING_GUIDE.md](WEEKLY_REPORT_WRITING_GUIDE.md) (full prose standards), [Translation blocks §](WEEKLY_REPORT_WRITING_GUIDE.md#translation-blocks-foreign-language-quotes), `.cursor/skills/draft-from-article-plan/SKILL.md` § Cult vs sect.

---

## Default rule

**Freedom Times voice defaults to *cult*** (or *high-control group* when *cult* would sound repetitive or legally loaded) for groups that are coercive, exploitative, violence-glorifying, or the subject of cult-specific reporting in the cited story.

European outlets often write *sect*, *secte*, *sekt*, or *Sekte*. **Do not carry those words into our English narration or translation folds** when *cult* accurately describes the reporting.

| Context | Word to use |
|---------|-------------|
| Our narration (English prose) | **cult** / **cult-like** |
| English translation fold | **cult** / **cult-like** |
| Original-language blockquote | Source word verbatim (*sekt*, *secte*, etc.) |
| Source citation headline | Unchanged (exact headline text) |
| Noting how an outlet labels a group | Our word **cult** + italic source label (*secte*, *sekt*) |

---

## When *cult* is correct

Use **cult** in our voice when the week’s story concerns:

- Sexual or physical abuse, extortion, or coercive control inside a group
- Violent or terror-linked networks (e.g. MKY, Com/764)
- Survivor testimony against a leader or community (e.g. Raëlian movement, Maasbach, Eternal Values)
- Specialist counselling or prosecution of high-control groups (infoSekta caseload)
- Movements classified or widely reported as exploitative or pseudoscientific (e.g. AllatRa — SBU classification, critic allegations)
- Documentary or book titles that use *cult* in English (*A Model Cult*, *Child of the Maasbach Cult*)

**Check the source:** if the outlet describes coercion, harm, criminal charges, survivor exit, or specialist cult counselling, *cult* is almost always the right English word for our sentences — even when the source headline says *sekt* or *secte*.

---

## When *cult* is wrong or needs care

| Situation | Use instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Canonical Catholic schism** (SSPX ordination, Vatican excommunication) | *traditionalist fraternity*, *schismatic act* — not *cult*; story is denomination/dispute |
| **Broad religious sociology** without coercion allegation (evangelical growth conference) | *evangelicals*, *movement*, *churches* — report Fath’s facts; only use *cult* when translating his *sectes* stereotype label in a translation fold |
| **Ordinary faith communities** with no harm allegation in the plan | Do not label them *cult* in our voice |
| **Legal presumption** | Attribute charges and labels to prosecutors; one clause on innocence where needed |

If the story is in the plan as cult news, there is usually a harm, control, or specialist-service angle — default *cult* unless the row above applies.

---

## *Sect* / *sekt* / *secte* — keep only here

1. **Original-language blockquote** — verbatim from the cited outlet  
   Example: `jag är med i en sekt` stays in the Swedish blockquote.

2. **Proper names** — do not translate  
   - infoSekta  
   - *Tunnelvisie: kind van de Maasbach-sekte* (book title)  
   - Sektenblog (watson.ch blog name in citations)

3. **Citation headlines** — exact headline in `## Source citations`  
   Example: `Åtalas för deltagande i terrorsekt` unchanged.

4. **Reporting a source’s label** — italic source term, our word still *cult*  
   - Good: “the cult … which *Le Figaro* labels *secte*”  
   - Bad: “the Raëlian sect” in our narration when we mean the movement generally

5. **Someone’s own word in English prose** — if quoting what they called it  
   Example: he called it “sekten 764” — keep *sekten* as his Swedish term in prose attribution.

## Source language → English (translation folds)

When converting foreign material to English in **translation blocks** or **translated narration**, use this mapping unless the cited outlet’s own **English** text deliberately uses *sect*:

| Source term | Language | English in our fold / narration |
|-------------|----------|----------------------------------|
| *sekt*, *sekten* | Swedish, Norwegian, Danish | **cult** |
| *secte*, *sectes* | French | **cult** / **cults** |
| *Sekte*, *sektenhaft* | German | **cult** / **cult-like** |
| *sect* | English outlet (e.g. E&E headline) | Note in narration: we say **cult**; outlet rendered SBU label as *sect* |

**Procedure:** read the source sentence → if it describes harm, control, violence, or specialist cult reporting → English fold uses **cult** → leave the foreign word only in the blockquote above the fold.

### Example (Com/764 — *Expressen*)

```markdown
> – … Alltså, jag är med i en sekt, fortsätter han i förhör.

<details class="translate">
<summary>Show English translation</summary>

… That is, I am in a cult, he continues in interrogation.

</details>
```

Swedish *sekt* in blockquote; English **cult** in fold. *Expressen* also describes 764 as a violent *internetsekt* — *cult* is correct.

### Example (Fath — *Charente Libre*)

```markdown
> « Pendant longtemps, on les associait à des sectes, surtout en France. »

<details class="translate">
<summary>Show English translation</summary>

For a long time they were associated with cults, especially in France.

</details>
```

French *sectes* in blockquote; English **cults** in fold (stereotype label Fath describes, not our classification of evangelicals as a cult).

## *High-control group* — when to use

Use **high-control group** sparingly as a variant when:

- *Cult* would repeat awkwardly in the same short section, or  
- The source emphasises control mechanics without using *cult* / *sekt*, or  
- Legal caution is needed alongside survivor-centred framing.

Do not replace every *cult* with *high-control group* — readers expect *cult* in **Europe & UK Cult News**.

---

## Quick decision checklist

Before publishing a section, ask:

- [ ] Is this **our English sentence**? → default **cult** unless SSPX-style schism or pure sociology without harm.
- [ ] Is this a **translation fold**? → **cult** / **cult-like** unless source English deliberately says *sect* in a quote you are preserving.
- [ ] Is this a **blockquote in the source language**? → keep *sekt* / *secte* verbatim.
- [ ] Is this a **citation headline**? → unchanged.
- [ ] Is this a **proper name** (infoSekta, book title)? → unchanged.
- [ ] Am I only **noting** how an outlet labels the group? → “the cult … which *X* labels *secte*”.
- [ ] Did I avoid **“the sect”** in our narration where **“the cult”** is accurate?

---

## Do not

- Mirror European tabloid *sect* in our English sentences when *cult* fits the reporting.
- Write *sect* in an English translation fold because the Swedish/French/German source used *sekt* / *secte*.
- Call SSPX or a mainstream evangelical conference a *cult* without harm allegations in the cited story.
- Lecturing readers on which traditions are “really” cults — report the week’s event and attributed labels.
