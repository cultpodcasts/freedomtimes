they rely on Stewards Foundation–style trusts. Let’s unpack this 
systematically.
1. Normal NSW Trust Law: The Baseline
Under the Trustee Act 1925 (NSW) and common law:
• All beneficiaries, whether present or future, must be treated fairly and have a right to 
information about the trust.
• Trustees owe fiduciary duties to every beneficiary — they can’t arbitrarily exclude past 
or former members if those people were within the beneficial class.
• Trust purposes must be certain and lawful; any trust excluding persons arbitrarily 
or discriminating without legal basis can be struck down as void for uncertainty or 
against public policy.
• Trustees must account — meaning transparent records, investments, and distributions 
are required.
So in normal NSW law, if a person was a beneficiary of a charitable or quasi-charitable 
trust, their interest can’t simply vanish because of a private “disciplinary” or “separation” 
rule — unless the trust deed lawfully says so and the rule isn’t unconscionable or ultra 
vires.
