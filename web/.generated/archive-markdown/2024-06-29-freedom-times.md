1. The Defendant Can Present 
His Case At The Trial 
This is the most basic and 
fundamental principle of justice. 
Even Pontius Pilate, the epitome 
of injustice, allowed Jesus the 
opportunity to speak in His 
defence! 
From JND through to JTJr, 
brethren were expected to be 
present at an “Assembly meeting” 
if their matters were being 
presented, and were invited to 
state their case. This is directly 
mandated by Matthew 18:17. 
This right was removed at 
some point in the Symington era. 
Assembly judgements are now 
pronounced and executed in the 
absence of the accused. 
A brother recently 
excommunicated in Leeds actually 
appealed to BDH to present his 
case to the assembly. He was 
refused on the grounds that “The 
brethren wouldn’t listen to you.” 
No right thinking person could 
possibly condone or excuse this 
blatant disregard of scripture and 
human rights. 
2. Witnesses 
2 Corinthians 13:1. “In the mouth 
of two or three witnesses shall 
every matter be established.” 
The scripture is unequivocal. 
There is no possible way that 
Paul could have meant “One 
witness”. He said two or three 
for a good reason – it prevents a 
powerful and ambitious individual 
(which Corinth had plenty of) from 
giving false witness to his own 
personal advantage. 
In 1968, JTJr said that one 
witness was sufficient for the 
priests to act. The effect of this 
was that a powerful and dishonest 
local, national or universal leader 
could be the accuser, the sole 
witness, the prosecution, judge 
and executioner in a matter, and 
thus falsely accuse, incriminate 
and withdraw from any potential 
rivals or persons that disagreed 
with them.. Notoriously, Henry 
Magahy of Bristol had a “hit list” 
of brethren to be eliminated - this 
was easily accomplished in the 
absence of any requirement for 
independent witness. 
3. Respect of Persons 
James 2 condemns “respect of 
persons” in the clearest possible 
language: 
“My brethren, do not have the 
faith of our Lord Jesus Christ, 
[Lord] of glory, with respect of

“Justice” is often represented

blindfolded woman, carrying a 
sword in one hand and scales in 
the other. The blindfold represents 
impartiality – it signifies that 
the same rules of justice will be 
applied without regard to who the 
defendant is. 
This can only happen where 
the judge has no conflict of 
interest in the case before him. 
Imagine that you had seriously 
injured a child in a car accident, 
and you find the judge assigned 
to your case is the father of the 
victim! Would you expect to 
receive a fair hearing?
Yet in the PBCC, there is 
frequently a blatant conflict of

closest family to a defective 
assembly judgement really be 
expected to deal impartially with 
the perpetrator? The Christian 
principles of justice would demand 
an impartial judge. The PBCC 
makes no such provision. Personal 
feeling is at the root of many of 
the harshest acts of injustice in the 
history of the brethren.
